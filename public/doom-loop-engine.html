How it runs. Each node carries a continuous stress level in [0,1] that relaxes each month toward a saturating function of its drive — exogenous spark (scenario-scaled) + weighted stress from upstream nodes + a scarcity term proportional to how far the buffer has fallen. The buffer is a real reservoir: a stress-weighted deficit is converted to a net drawdown in M bbl/d (via the draw-scale bridge), throttled by demand destruction, and integrated down month by month. Allocation fires when the drawable cushion hits zero. The loop. The node graph itself is feed-forward (no cycles), so its standalone gain is zero — the entire feedback loop is node → buffer → node: stress draws the cushion down, a thinner cushion raises the scarcity term, which raises stress. So instead of a node eigenvalue we report the scarcity loop gain (how much the net draw accelerates from full cushion to the floor) and the net draw at the floor in M bbl/d. If demand destruction pulls net draw to ≤ 0 at maximum scarcity, an interior buffer level holds (self-limiting); if it stays positive, no equilibrium exists above the floor and the cushion empties — a threshold, bounded by the buffer hitting zero, not a runaway to infinity. Bands: Broken = cushion breached; Strained = more than half the cushion eaten without breach; Absorbed = the system held. Every edge weight, draw weight, the draw-scale bridge and the buffer parameters are subjective inputs — change them and argue with the result.

Calibrating the cushion. "Drawable" has no single value — it depends on definition. OECD commercial stocks stood at ~2.84 billion bbl (Jan 2026); OECD government + obligated strategic stocks add ~1.85 billion bbl; but the amount realistically drawable before operational stress (≈20%-minimum tank bottoms, pipeline fill, ~65% refinery minimums) is far smaller — a May 2026 estimate put it near ~0.8 billion bbl. This tool defaults to 0.8 (the conservative operational figure) and lets the cushion slider span up toward the strategic-inclusive range. The cushion is also a moving target: global observed inventories hit a multi-year high (~8.2 billion bbl) in early 2026 and have been drawn down since.
