# Euro Oil Watch Weekly

## Week of 2 July 2026

### Oil Prices Fall, but Hormuz Remains a Contested and Conditional Passage

European oil markets have received considerable price relief. Brent crude traded near **$71 per barrel on 2 July**, while West Texas Intermediate fell to around **$68**, returning both benchmarks to their lowest levels since 27 February.

The fall reflects progress in indirect U.S.-Iran negotiations, the gradual release of oil trapped inside the Gulf and the restart of exports from major terminals such as Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura. However, the market price is giving a more reassuring picture than the physical shipping situation warrants.

The Strait of Hormuz is carrying more vessels, but it is not operating normally. Iran continues to assert authority over the routes ships must use, which vessels may pass and whether compulsory fees can eventually be imposed. Recent attacks have shown that departures from Iran's preferred arrangements can still trigger military action.

The central question is therefore no longer simply whether ships can leave the Gulf.

It is whether enough empty tankers will return, load new cargoes and repeat the journey under commercially acceptable conditions.

That has not yet been demonstrated.

## Market Snapshot

**Brent crude:** Approximately $70.91 per barrel on the morning of 2 July
**WTI crude:** Approximately $67.99 per barrel
**Venezuelan exports:** 1.25 million barrels per day in May
**Venezuelan exports to Europe:** Approximately 169,000 barrels per day
**U.S. LNG delivered to Europe in June:** 4.41 million tonnes, or just under 42% of U.S. exports

Prices are clearly improving, but the physical recovery remains incomplete and highly uneven.

## 1. Ships Are Leaving the Gulf — But Will They Return?

The sharp drop in crude prices has been driven partly by previously stranded oil finally reaching the market.

Kpler estimated in mid-June that approximately **93 million barrels of non-Iranian crude** were stranded inside the Gulf, while **Vortexa** separately counted around **87 million barrels aboard 54 supertankers**. A substantial part of that backlog is now moving, though the precise volume depends on the tracker and methodology used. **Reuters reported on 2 July, citing LSEG and Kpler vessel data,** that five VLCCs carrying a combined 10 million barrels of Saudi crude had loaded at Ras Tanura and exited Hormuz, with further tankers positioned near the terminal waiting to load.

This is genuine progress — but it does not yet amount to a fully functioning export system.

**Lloyd's List data cited by Reuters** recorded **242 vessel transits** through Hormuz in the week to 28 June, including **57 crude-tanker crossings**, compared with more than **700 total weekly crossings** before the conflict. Most of the observed movement was eastbound, reflecting loaded ships and delayed cargoes leaving the Gulf rather than a fully restored two-way shipping cycle.

These figures should be treated cautiously: they count vessel movements rather than confirmed export volumes and rely on proprietary tracking methodologies, and other public indicators continue to show crude throughput at only around half its pre-conflict level.

A normal shipping cycle requires a comparable flow of empty — or ballast — tankers travelling westward into the Gulf to collect the next round of cargoes. Before the war, the Gulf contained around 150 million barrels of available crude-tanker capacity, much of it aboard empty vessels waiting to load. That capacity has fallen below 100 million barrels, with relatively few freely available ballast tankers.

Without sufficient returning ships, Gulf producers cannot confidently restore production. Oil placed into storage eventually has nowhere to go, forcing producers to reduce output again.

The stronger evidence of normalisation will be sustained westbound movement by empty tankers, followed by repeated loadings and outbound voyages. The oil currently reaching the market may represent the clearing of a backlog rather than the beginning of sustained normal trade.

## 2. Iran Is Attempting to Establish a New Transit Regime

The June agreement permits vessels to pass without charges for 60 days, but Washington and Tehran appear to interpret the arrangement very differently.

The United States regards Hormuz as an international waterway through which commercial vessels must have free passage. Iran, by contrast, argues that it retains the right to decide:

* which vessels may enter or leave the Gulf;
* which routes those vessels must follow;
* whether a vessel presents a threat to Iranian security;
* and whether ships must eventually pay for navigation, security or other compulsory services.

Iranian sources have said that, unless another agreement is reached, charges could begin in **mid-August**. No final fee schedule has yet been published.

The disagreement is not theoretical. Iran reportedly fired on four vessels that attempted to use the Omani side of the strait without first obtaining Iranian permission. On 2 July, Iran's military warned tankers to follow approved routes and navigation procedures or face an immediate and forceful response.

Iran is therefore attempting to transform Hormuz from a freely navigable international passage into a managed transit system in which Tehran exercises effective approval over commercial movement.

The United States and Gulf governments reject that interpretation. The dispute remains one of the principal points capable of restarting the conflict.

## 3. What About Ships Connected to Israel?

Reports that "Israeli ships have been banned from the Persian Gulf" require careful qualification.

Iran has previously said that vessels **linked to Israel or the United States** may not cross the Strait of Hormuz. However, Tehran has not provided a transparent definition of what constitutes such a link.

It could potentially refer to:

* the ship's flag;
* registered or beneficial ownership;
* the operator or charterer;
* the cargo owner;
* a previous port call;
* or the vessel's eventual destination.

Enforcement has not been uniform, and the full scale of the restriction cannot be independently established. Nevertheless, the uncertainty itself creates a serious commercial risk. A shipowner may not know whether Iran considers a vessel Israeli- or American-linked until the ship approaches the strait.

This ambiguity will discourage some owners, insurers and charterers from returning, even when crude prices and freight rates make a voyage financially attractive.

## 4. The Threat of Military Escalation Has Not Disappeared

The fragility of the reopening was demonstrated in late June when commercial vessels were attacked close to the Omani side of Hormuz.

Following one incident, the United States struck Iranian missile, drone and coastal-radar facilities. Iran retaliated against American-linked targets in the region before the two sides agreed to halt further attacks and return to negotiations.

The immediate escalation was contained, but the episode showed how quickly the interim arrangement can break down.

Traffic also slowed rapidly after the attack. According to Reuters, tanker movements fell from 27 transits on 24 June to 13 on 26 June. Overall traffic had reached 62 vessels on 24 June, the highest daily figure since the conflict began, but still only around half the level recorded on the same date in 2025.

Before the conflict, Hormuz averaged approximately 125 ship crossings per day.

The message is clear: a few days of rising traffic do not establish a reliable trend. One attack, disputed route or military misunderstanding can cause commercial movements to fall again almost immediately.

## 5. Freight Costs Still Signal Serious Danger

Transport costs provide another warning against declaring the crisis over.

The TD3C freight index, which tracks the cost of moving crude from the Middle East to China, has fallen by almost 40% since the ceasefire. The **Baltic Exchange's Week 26 assessment** put TD3C-equivalent VLCC earnings at close to **$313,000 per day**, and **Reuters subsequently cited a rate of around $287,000 on 30 June** — still extraordinarily high compared with a long-term average materially below $100,000.

War-risk insurance is available, but prices, exclusions and voyage conditions remain volatile. Owners must also consider mines, electronic interference, routing disputes, possible sanctions exposure and the risk that Iran may change its conditions while a vessel is already approaching the strait.

Until freight rates and insurance conditions move much closer to historical levels, the shipping market cannot be considered normal.

## 6. Lower Crude Does Not Automatically Mean Cheap European Fuel

European refiners are benefiting from the fall in crude prices, but the outlook for diesel, jet fuel and gasoline remains more complicated.

The original Hormuz disruption affected refined-product exports as well as crude. Global product exports were estimated to be approximately **4 million barrels per day lower year on year**, driven by missing Gulf exports, weaker Asian refining activity and around 2.5 million barrels per day of war-related Middle Eastern refinery outages.

Crude flows can recover before refined-product supply does.

This means European refiners may receive cheaper feedstock while diesel and jet-fuel markets remain comparatively tight. Refiners capable of running at high utilisation could benefit from the gap between falling crude costs and still-elevated product prices.

Goldman Sachs has forecast European diesel refining margins of approximately **$37 per barrel during the fourth quarter of 2026**, with European gasoline margins around $14. These are forecasts rather than current realised margins, but they illustrate the continuing tightness expected in refined fuels.

For European consumers, the likely outcome is a lagged and uneven improvement:

* crude-price pressure is easing;
* refinery economics may remain supportive;
* wholesale fuel prices should soften;
* but diesel, aviation fuel and some industrial products may remain expensive relative to crude.

Europe could therefore receive cheaper oil before it receives abundant diesel.

## 7. LNG Recovery Is Lagging Behind Oil

The LNG market is recovering more slowly than the crude market.

Seven empty QatarEnergy-linked LNG carriers travelled westward into the Gulf between 11 and 22 June. These were the first substantial returning ballast movements since the conflict began and are an encouraging indication that Qatar's export cycle may be restarting.

However, analysts had still not observed a broad return of empty Qatari and UAE LNG vessels. Mines continued to prevent normal use of the main pre-war shipping lane, and LNG movements remained only a fraction of previous levels.

Shell estimates that Hormuz disruption has shut in roughly one-fifth of global monthly LNG supply since the conflict began. Even if flows return to normal during the next three months, global LNG trade could remain broadly flat in 2026 rather than recording the growth previously expected.

Europe also faces increasing competition for Atlantic LNG.

Only **4.41 million tonnes**, or just under 42%, of U.S. LNG exports went to Europe in June, down from 5.13 million tonnes and just over 50% in May. Stronger prices in Asia and record demand from Egypt encouraged exporters to redirect cargoes away from Europe.

European buyers still need to rebuild storage before winter, but some have delayed purchases in anticipation of lower prices later in the year. That strategy could become risky if Hormuz suffers another disruption or Asian demand strengthens.

## 8. Venezuela Offers Europe Useful Diversification—With Limits

Venezuela's oil recovery gives Europe an important source of additional Atlantic Basin supply.

The country exported approximately **1.25 million barrels per day in May**, its third consecutive monthly increase and 61% more than a year earlier. Around 169,000 barrels per day went to Europe, while the United States and India remained the two largest destinations.

The Venezuelan government is targeting production of 1.37 million barrels per day by the end of 2026.

Venezuelan crude is nevertheless not a direct substitute for every lost Middle Eastern barrel. Much of the country's production consists of heavy or extra-heavy crude requiring complex refining equipment. Suitable refineries can gain valuable feedstock, but simpler plants may be unable to process it efficiently.

Venezuelan logistics also remain dependent on imported diluent. The country imported approximately 93,000 barrels per day of heavy naphtha in May to blend with its extra-heavy crude and make it transportable.

Venezuela should therefore be viewed as a growing source of diversification, particularly for complex European refineries, rather than a universal replacement for Gulf supply.

## What to Watch

### Returning ballast tankers

Loaded ships leaving the Gulf clear accumulated inventories. Empty ships returning to load new cargoes demonstrate that owners expect trade to continue. Westbound ballast traffic is now the most important measure of genuine recovery.

### Iranian routing demands

Watch whether vessels can use the Omani side of the strait without Iranian permission. Continued Iranian attempts to control both sides would keep the risk of military confrontation high.

### Fees after the 60-day period

The interim toll-free arrangement could expire in mid-August. Any Iranian attempt to impose compulsory charges would be rejected by Washington and could become the next major flashpoint.

### Freight and insurance

A meaningful fall in tanker rates, war-risk premiums and contractual exclusions would show that commercial confidence is returning.

### Refined-product exports

Europe needs diesel, jet fuel and petrochemical feedstocks as well as crude. Product flows must recover before the wider fuel market can be regarded as secure.

### LNG ships returning to Qatar

A consistent flow of empty LNG carriers into the Gulf, followed by fully loaded ships leaving Qatar, would provide stronger evidence of normalisation than occasional individual voyages.

## Bottom Line

Oil prices have moved decisively in Europe's favour, but the Strait of Hormuz remains far from safe, settled or commercially normal.

The current fall in Brent has been accelerated by a temporary surge of previously trapped crude entering the market. That creates a near-term supply glut, but it does not prove that a sustainable shipping cycle has been restored.

Iran is still claiming authority over vessel approvals, designated routes and future fees. Ships linked to Israel or the United States may face additional restrictions, while attacks on vessels using the Omani side have demonstrated that Tehran is willing to enforce its demands militarily.

The decisive test is not how many tankers leave during a temporary opening. It is whether owners repeatedly send empty ships back into the Gulf, load fresh cargoes and sail out again without political interference or attack.

Europe has gained price relief.

It has not yet regained energy security.
