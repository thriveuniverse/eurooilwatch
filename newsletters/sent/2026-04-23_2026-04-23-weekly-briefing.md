**EuroOilWatch Weekly**
**Thursday, 23 April 2026**

Welcome to this edition of EuroOilWatch Weekly.

The European picture remains under real strain. EuroOilWatch's dashboard, updated early on 23 April 2026, shows EU average reserves at **99 days for petrol**, **98 days for diesel**, and **92 days for jet fuel**, while **22 of 27 reporting countries are below the 90-day benchmark** for at least one fuel type. The same dashboard shows Brent crude at **$103.45/barrel**, EU average petrol at **€1.764/litre**, and EU average diesel at **€1.943/litre**. ([EuroOilWatch](https://eurooilwatch.com/))

**This week's takeaway:** Europe is not looking at a clean oil-price story. It is looking at a broader fuel-security story. Brent is back above $100, shipping through the Strait of Hormuz remains heavily disrupted, and Europe's pressure points are becoming clearer: diesel, jet fuel, and the uneven reserve picture between member states. ([EuroOilWatch](https://eurooilwatch.com/))

## Three numbers that matter

### Brent crude — $103.45/barrel

That is the number setting the tone across the continent again. Reuters reported Brent at $103.17 on Thursday as U.S.–Iran talks made no progress and shipping through Hormuz remained disrupted. EuroOilWatch's dashboard was showing $103.45 shortly afterwards. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/))

### EU average diesel — €1.943/litre

Diesel remains the clearest day-to-day pressure point for freight, logistics, agriculture, and industrial users across Europe. The continent's average has eased from the worst levels seen earlier this month, but it is still elevated against a market backdrop that remains highly unstable. ([EuroOilWatch](https://eurooilwatch.com/))

### 22 of 27 reporting countries below benchmark somewhere

That is the figure that matters most when headline EU averages start to look deceptively calm. Europe's problem is not just price. It is uneven resilience. Some countries still have relatively comfortable buffers; others do not. ([EuroOilWatch](https://eurooilwatch.com/))

## What is happening now

Reuters reported on 23 April that Iran seized two vessels in the Strait of Hormuz, while both Washington and Tehran kept restrictions in place on maritime traffic. Hormuz normally carries nearly 20% of global oil trade, which is why the market has turned higher again even without a new full-scale military escalation. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/))

For Europe, that matters because the physical market is still much tighter than the futures market suggests. Reuters reported on 13 April that physical Forties crude for prompt delivery to Europe reached **$148.87/barrel**, while diesel **wholesale cargoes** (not forecourt prices) delivered to North West Europe were around **$170/barrel**. In other words, Europe may see a Brent quote near $103, but the barrels and products it actually needs can still trade at much harsher levels. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/))

## Europe's response is getting more serious

The European Commission published a new package of measures on 22 April aimed at offsetting surging energy prices. Reuters reported that Brussels wants to ensure electricity is taxed below natural gas, coordinate possible oil-stock releases, map refining capacity, and monitor transport-fuel supply more closely. The Commission is also preparing temporary state-aid rules that would let governments subsidise up to **50% of fuel or fertiliser price increases** paid since the Iran war began. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/))

Jet fuel is becoming a particular point of concern. Reuters reported on 22 April that the EU is considering requiring countries to hold specific jet-fuel stockpiles and even redistribute supply regionally if shortages worsen. The International Energy Agency has forecast shortages by June if Europe can replace only half of the jet fuel it normally gets from the Middle East. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/))

That matters beyond aviation. Reuters also reported this week that the fuel-price shock has added about **€88 ($104)** in fuel cost per passenger on long-haul flights leaving Europe, and **€29** on flights within Europe. This is another sign that the disruption is spreading well beyond crude headlines into real transport costs. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/))

## Germany and 1 May — another warning sign for Europe

One important development for the wider European fuel picture arrives on 1 May. Reuters reported that Russia will stop the supply of Kazakh crude oil via the Druzhba pipeline to Germany from that date, affecting the PCK Schwedt refinery, which supplies most of Berlin's fuel and relies on Kazakhstan for about **17% of its crude supply**. Germany says petroleum-product security is not in jeopardy because alternative routes exist, but its own regulator warned there could still be regional pricing effects. ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/))

That does not mean Germany is about to run out of oil. It does mean Europe is adding another layer of regional supply friction at exactly the wrong moment.
