**EuroOilWatch Weekly Briefing**
**Thursday 18 June 2026**

---

## The headline

Europe finally has some oil-price relief.

Brent crude has fallen sharply after the US and Iran signed an interim agreement aimed at ending the war, reopening the Strait of Hormuz and restoring Gulf oil flows. The market has moved from panic pricing to de-escalation pricing.

That matters. A lower Brent price should reduce pressure on European wholesale fuel, freight costs and inflation expectations. But this is not an all-clear.

Europe's problem is no longer simply the headline oil price. It is the thinness of the physical system underneath it: diesel, jet fuel, refinery margins, shipping insurance, uneven national reserves and the lag before lower crude reaches pump prices.

The correct read this week is: **the war premium is easing; the fuel-security cushion is still weak.**

---

## This week's EuroOilWatch dashboard

**Brent:** around **$79.6/bbl** on the EuroOilWatch dashboard (Reuters reported ~$77.41 intraday on 18 June after the interim agreement) — the lowest since early March.

**Reserve cover — March 2026 (26 of 27 EU states reporting), two ways of counting:**

- **Petrol:** 96 days (EU country average) · **79 days (stock-weighted)**
- **Diesel:** 88 days (country average) · **82 days (stock-weighted)**
- **Jet fuel:** 87 days (country average) · **59 days (stock-weighted)**

Those two columns matter. The *country average* treats all 27 members equally; the *stock-weighted* figure — total EU stock ÷ total EU consumption — is the truer "how many days does Europe physically hold," and it is **lower across the board because the largest-consuming states hold less cover than the small, high-cover holders**. On the physically meaningful basis, even petrol (79 days) sits below 90, and **jet fuel is only ~59 days**.

A note on the benchmark: the formal EU/IEA obligation is **90 days of net imports or 61 days of consumption, whichever is higher**. The figures above are days of consumption, so they clear the 61-day consumption floor — but on the dashboard's stricter 90-day reference line, diesel and jet fuel sit below, and the stock-weighted picture is thinner still.

The average also hides a very uneven map: **diesel cover is critically low in 15 of 27 countries**, including Romania, Slovakia and Luxembourg, while a handful of high-cover states pull the simple average up.

That is why EuroOilWatch stays in **caution / fragile-recovery mode** rather than declaring the crisis over.

---

## Fuel prices: easing, but still expensive

The latest EC Weekly Oil Bulletin (8 June) puts EU average **petrol at €1.786/litre and diesel at €1.804/litre** — easing from the spring spike, but still high by normal consumer standards.

Diesel remains the key stress point because it feeds directly into road haulage, food distribution, agriculture, construction, emergency logistics and industrial supply chains.

The next question is pass-through. If Brent stays in the high-$70s or low-$80s, pump prices should soften further. If diesel stays sticky, it will tell us the physical middle-distillate market remains tight even after the crude-price correction.

---

## Why the market changed

The US–Iran memorandum has changed the psychology of the oil market. Two weeks ago, traders were pricing the risk that Hormuz would remain blocked or unstable. This week, they are pricing the possibility that Iranian and Gulf barrels return faster than expected. That has pulled Brent down to its lowest level since early March.

But the physical recovery will not be instant. Tanker schedules, shipping insurance, port confidence, mine-clearance questions, refinery crude slates and product-routing decisions all take longer to repair than futures prices.

So the framing has shifted from *"war premium back / tight Hormuz"* to *"de-escalation premium easing / physical rebuild still incomplete."*

---

## Diesel is still Europe's warning light

Diesel remains the fuel to watch. Europe's economy is more diesel-sensitive than households often realise: a diesel squeeze hits lorries, supermarket logistics, farms, construction sites, fishing fleets, local authorities and emergency services before it appears as a general consumer problem.

Goldman Sachs has warned that diesel and gasoline margins could remain elevated through 2026 even as crude falls, because demand can recover faster than refined-product supply. (That is a bank forecast, not a settled fact — but it fits Europe's position, having spent years re-routing diesel away from Russia toward the Middle East, India, the US and other refining hubs.)

A falling Brent price helps. It does not automatically solve diesel.

---

## Jet fuel: Europe is rebuilding routes

Reuters reports that Indian jet-fuel exports to Europe have rebounded toward pre-ban levels, with recent EU imports around the **low-60,000 barrels-per-day range** (the highest since December 2025). That is a positive sign for Europe's aviation system after the disruption from the Russian-origin fuel ban and the Hormuz shock.

But it also shows the new shape of Europe's vulnerability. Europe is not simply short of crude; it is dependent on complex product routes — Indian refining, Middle East logistics, tanker availability, sanctions compliance and Mediterranean import chains. For aviation, the supply chain is healing, but not simple.

---

## Russia and the shadow fleet

Europe's oil-security story is still tied to Russia. The EU has expanded sanctions against Russian shadow-fleet figures and related networks; that tightens enforcement, but also adds friction to maritime oil flows, insurance checks and cargo screening.

For EuroOilWatch this is a background risk rather than a direct pump-price trigger this week. The bigger point: Europe's fuel system is now shaped by two overlapping disruptions — the Hormuz shock and the post-Russian-import restructuring of refined-product trade. Even with Hormuz easing, the sanctions-routing problem remains.

---

## Gas matters too

Although EuroOilWatch focuses on oil and fuel, the gas market still shapes the wider energy picture. Reuters notes Europe's gas market survived the Hormuz shock better than feared, thanks to US LNG and increased supply from Algeria and Nigeria — but prices still rose sharply during the conflict and the EU's energy bill widened materially. High gas prices feed into industry, fertiliser, power costs and household pressure. Oil is easing; Europe's total energy stress has not disappeared.

---

## EuroOilWatch view

This is the first genuinely better week in the crisis. The oil-price ceiling has come down, and the market is no longer pricing an immediate worsening of Hormuz. If the memorandum holds, Europe should see more crude and product relief over the summer.

But the floor under risk remains. Diesel and jet-fuel reserves are below the benchmark — and on the stock-weighted basis even petrol is below 90 days and jet fuel is near 59. Several member states remain badly exposed. Product margins are still vulnerable. Shipping normalisation will take time. Pump prices will lag crude.

So the correct dashboard language is: **improving price picture, fragile fuel-security base.** Europe is moving from the emergency phase into the repair phase — progress, but the repair phase still needs actual barrels, actual tankers, actual inventories and actual lower pump prices.

---

## What to watch next

1. **Brent pass-through** — does the fall in Brent reach European petrol and diesel, or do pump prices stay sticky?
2. **Diesel reserve cover** — does it move back above benchmark in the next Eurostat update, on *both* the country-average and stock-weighted measures?
3. **Jet-fuel imports** — do Indian and Middle Eastern routes keep rebuilding, or do sanctions and shipping frictions slow it?
4. **Hormuz physical traffic** — the market has moved; tanker flows now need to confirm the political story. Note the satellite-AIS transit data publishes with roughly a week's lag, so it confirms a real reopening *after the fact*, not in real time — watch it over the coming weeks, not days.
5. **Shadow-fleet enforcement** — new sanctions may improve enforcement but add maritime friction.
6. **Country-level vulnerability** — watch Romania, Slovakia, Luxembourg and other low-cover states rather than relying on the EU average.

**Bottom line:** Europe's oil shock is easing, but its fuel-security problem has not vanished. Brent is falling because the Hormuz risk premium is being stripped out. Diesel and jet fuel — and, on a stock-weighted basis, even petrol — remain the real test of whether Europe is actually safe, not just temporarily relieved.
