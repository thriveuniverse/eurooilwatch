This week: Brent slips below $100, but Europe's physical diesel market tells a different story.

EuroOilWatch's dashboard was updated on 14 April 2026 and now shows EU average reserve cover of 97 days for petrol, 98 days for diesel, and 91 days for jet fuel, with 22 of 27 reporting countries below the 90-day benchmark for at least one fuel type.

Brent has slipped back below $100, but that is not an all-clear for Europe. The latest dashboard shows Brent crude at $98.30/barrel, EU average petrol at €1.796/litre, and EU average diesel at €2.040/litre. Diesel remains the clearest pressure point because Europe is structurally short of it and still exposed to replacement-cargo stress.

Three numbers that matter

Brent crude — $98.30/barrel
The crude price has eased, but it is still trading at a level that reflects serious geopolitical risk rather than normal market conditions.

EU average diesel — €2.040/litre
Diesel remains the most useful pressure gauge for freight, logistics, agriculture, and industrial users across the bloc.

22 of 27 countries below benchmark
That is the number that matters most when headline EU averages start to look deceptively calm. Europe's overall picture is not one of uniform security. It is one of uneven resilience.

Middle East update — why the risk has not gone away

Reuters reported on 14 April that the U.S. blockade of Iranian ports began, while oil prices eased on hopes that diplomacy might still resume. A Chinese-owned tanker managed to pass through the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday, which suggests limited movement is possible, but not that the route is back to normal. Britain and France have both said they will not participate in the blockade and have instead stressed the need to reopen the waterway. Reuters →

For Europe, the danger is not just the headline futures price. Reuters also reported that physical crude cargoes for immediate delivery into Europe hit record highs near $150/barrel, North West Europe diesel cargoes were around $170/barrel, and Europe remains structurally short of both diesel and jet fuel. Kpler data cited by Reuters said that in 2025, the EU-27 plus the UK sourced 42% of diesel imports and 62% of jet fuel imports from the Middle East. Reuters →

That is why Brent slipping below $100 should not be mistaken for genuine relief. Europe may get some breathing room in futures markets while still paying much more for the physical barrels and refined fuels it actually needs. That gap is the real warning sign.

Policy angle — Brussels is already reacting

The European Commission said on 13 April that it wants to loosen state-aid rules temporarily so governments can provide more support to sectors hit by fuel-price spikes, including agriculture, road transport, and shipping. Germany, Italy, Poland, and Hungary have already introduced national measures such as fuel-price caps and tax cuts. Reuters →
