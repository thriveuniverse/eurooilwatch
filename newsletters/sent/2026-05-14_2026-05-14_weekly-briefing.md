**EuroOilWatch Weekly**
**Thursday, 14 May 2026**

Welcome to this week's EuroOilWatch briefing.

The key message this morning is that Europe's fuel picture looks calmer on the screen than it does underneath. On the EuroOilWatch dashboard, updated **13 May 2026 at 07:45**, **15 of 27 EU countries** still hold critical reserves in at least one fuel type. The site shows **EU average reserves at 113 days for petrol, 102 days for diesel, and 95 days for jet fuel**, with **Brent at $106.68/barrel**, **EU average petrol at €1.809/litre**, and **EU average diesel at €1.934/litre**. The European Commission's latest Weekly Oil Bulletin was published today, **14 May 2026**, confirming that the official weekly retail-price update has now refreshed again.

## This week's takeaway

Last week's story was about whether the market had found a temporary floor after the first shock. This week's story is harsher: the market may look less panicked, but the physical system is still under real strain. Reuters reported today that the oil market is in a "deceptive calm," with the disruption cushioned so far by a surge in U.S. exports, a pullback in Chinese buying, and heavy stock draws. The IEA's May Oil Market Report, published yesterday, shows inventories drawing at record pace and the market structurally undersupplied through Q3 even if the conflict ends in early June.

## Three numbers that matter

**Brent crude — $106.68/barrel.** That is the current EuroOilWatch dashboard reading. Reuters separately reported that Brent futures were still rising early on **14 May**, as traders watched the Trump–Xi meeting for any sign of movement on the Iran war and the still-restricted Strait of Hormuz.

**EU average diesel — €1.934/litre.** Diesel remains the clearest pain point for the real economy. EuroOilWatch still shows diesel reserves at **102 days** on average across the EU — above the site's 90-day benchmark, but only modestly so, which means Europe is not out of diesel but is also not operating with comfortable slack.

**15 of 27 countries in critical territory somewhere.** That is the number that matters most. The site's headline warning says **15 of 27 countries** remain critical in at least one fuel category. The dashboard also flags jet fuel as the weakest major product line, and its jet-fuel tracker shows an EU average of **95.1 days**, with **13 of 27 countries** classed as critical and the Amsterdam-Rotterdam-Antwerp hub at **600 kt**, down **7.6% week on week** and at a six-year low.

## What changed since last week

The IEA's May 2026 Oil Market Report puts hard numbers on the scale of the Hormuz shock. Output from Gulf countries affected by the Strait closure was **14.4 million barrels per day below pre-war levels**, while cumulative Gulf producer supply losses had exceeded **1 billion barrels by mid-May** — although the net market gap is smaller because inventories, non-Gulf exports and demand destruction have partly offset the shock. **Global observed inventories fell by 246 million barrels across March and April**, based on preliminary IEA data, and the IEA now expects the market to be **1.78 mb/d undersupplied in 2026**, remaining short through Q3 even if the conflict ends in early June. In Europe's jet fuel market, **Middle East arrivals collapsed from 330,000 b/d in March to 60,000 b/d in April** — an **82% month-on-month fall** — leaving Europe scrambling to replace lost aviation fuel supply. Separately, OPEC's own May MOMR cut its 2026 demand-growth forecast as well, broadly aligning with the IEA on direction if not magnitude.

## What stands out on EuroOilWatch

The clearest regional stress signal on the site right now is the **Druzhba disruption into Germany**. EuroOilWatch notes that since **1 May 2026**, Russia has halted Kazakh crude supply via the Druzhba pipeline to Germany, leaving the **PCK Schwedt refinery** with about **17% less crude**. Germany says product security is not in jeopardy because alternative routes exist, but both Reuters and the site note the risk of regional pricing effects. In plain English: this is not Europe-wide physical failure, but it is exactly the kind of extra friction that makes an already stressed system less forgiving.

The second standout is that Europe is moving into storage-refill season without much strategic comfort. EuroOilWatch's latest lead analysis piece is bluntly titled **"Europe Heads Into Storage Refill Season With No Plan B for Iranian Oil"**, and that is probably the right framing. The site's analysis section is also still carrying strong, highly topical pieces on **Europe's exposure to a global oil supply shock**, **the real implications of a prolonged Hormuz disruption**, and **Ireland's energy-security vulnerability**.

## What to watch next week

Watch **Hormuz and diplomacy** first. Reuters reported this morning that markets are focused on whether the Trump–Xi meeting produces any movement on the Iran war. If it does not, the current "wait-and-see" mood could give way to another leg higher in prices.

Watch **jet fuel** second. Europe has not fully replaced the lost Middle East volumes, and summer aviation demand is approaching. Even without a dramatic shortage headline, that is an obvious pressure point for prices and regional logistics.

Watch **North West European refining and regional pricing** third. Schwedt's crude loss is not, by itself, a continental emergency, but Europe is already running with thin margins. Additional local disruption is exactly how a broad market stress starts showing up in specific places.

## Final word

Europe is not in a retail-fuel panic. But it is also not back to normal.

Brent is still above $100. Diesel is still expensive. Jet-fuel replacement is incomplete. Inventories globally are being drawn down at record pace. And Europe is carrying regional refinery and logistics friction on top of all that. This is why the current market should not be mistaken for stability. It is better described as a temporary balance held together by stock draws, rerouted trade, and hope that diplomacy catches up before summer demand does.
