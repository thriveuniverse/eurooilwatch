**EuroOilWatch Weekly**
**Thursday, 21 May 2026**

Welcome to this week's EuroOilWatch briefing.

The headline number has improved a little, but the underlying system still looks tight. EuroOilWatch, updated on **21 May**, says **19 of 27 EU countries** still hold critical reserves in at least one fuel type. The site shows EU average reserves at **113 days for petrol**, **102 days for diesel** and **96 days for jet fuel**, while current market prices are **$105.64 Brent**, **€1.824/litre for EU petrol** and **€1.863/litre for EU diesel**. EuroOilWatch also estimates physical Northwest European crude at **€140/bbl ($152)**, or **44% above Brent futures**, which is a reminder that the physical market remains much tighter than the paper price suggests. The European Commission's latest Weekly Oil Bulletin page was updated on 21 May. (EuroOilWatch; European Commission)

## This week's takeaway

Europe is no longer in the first wave of panic, but it is not back to normal either. Reuters reported on 21 May that Brent rebounded **up 1.3% to $106.41 by 1037 GMT**, after the **previous day's 5.6% fall**, with traders still reacting to every headline around U.S.–Iran talks, persistent supply tightness and large U.S. inventory drawdowns. Reuters also noted that before the war, the Strait of Hormuz handled oil and LNG shipments equal to roughly **20% of global consumption**, which is why even partial disruption there continues to support prices and keep nerves on edge. (Reuters)

## Three numbers that matter

**Brent crude — $105.64/barrel.** That is where EuroOilWatch has it this morning. It is down from the worst spikes, but still far above late-February levels, and Reuters says the market remains highly sensitive to whether Iran talks produce genuine de-escalation or another disappointment. (EuroOilWatch; Reuters)

**EU average diesel — €1.863/litre.** Diesel remains the most practical pressure point for Europe's real economy. EuroOilWatch's current reserve average is **102 days**, only modestly above the 90-day benchmark, and its active disruption warning says the latest German refinery friction is tightening North West European refining and diesel balances at the worst possible moment. (EuroOilWatch)

**Jet fuel — 96.4 days EU average.** EuroOilWatch's jet tracker says **13 of 27 countries are critical**, with **Poland the most stressed at 25.3 days**. That makes jet the most visibly fragile major fuel line on the site right now. (EuroOilWatch)

## What changed this week

The market tone has shifted from relief back to caution. Reuters said on 21 May that oil rebounded because supply tightness and rapid inventory drawdowns are making it difficult for prices to stay low for long, even after yesterday's drop. Iran has also announced a new **Persian Gulf Strait Authority** and described a **"controlled maritime zone"** in the Strait of Hormuz, while most of the fighting has paused without normal traffic through the strait being restored. In other words, the war premium has eased a little, but the logistics premium has not. (Reuters)

The bigger warning is that time is no longer on the market's side. Reuters' **Open Interest** columnist Ron Bousso warned today that the oil market may have only **about three months before tightening supplies begin to bite in earnest**, arguing that IEA assumptions of a **late-May reopening and June traffic resumption** now look optimistic. (Reuters Open Interest column; IEA Oil Market Report, May 2026)

## Why Europe still looks vulnerable

The best example is jet fuel. Reuters reported on 13 May that Europe's Middle East jet-fuel arrivals fell from **330,000 bpd in March to just 60,000 bpd in April**, and that Europe replaced only **70% of March's import level** in April. Imports from the U.S. and Nigeria **reached 221,000 bpd** in April per Kpler data cited by Reuters, but not enough to fully close the gap. That matters because Europe is heading into the summer travel season with inventories already drawing and the replacement supply chains doing more work than they were built for. (Reuters; IEA Oil Market Report, May 2026; Kpler)

That stress is now visible in the real economy. Reuters reported today that easyJet's half-year results show jet fuel prices up **more than 80% since late February**, a **£552 million first-half loss**, and a capacity shift **toward shorter domestic and city routes as longer-haul eastern Mediterranean demand weakens**. Even if Europe avoids outright physical shortages at major airports, the cost and behavioural effects are already showing up. (Reuters; easyJet H1 results)

## The regional friction point to watch

EuroOilWatch's active disruption banner is still pointing to the right secondary risk. Since **1 May 2026**, Russia has halted Kazakh crude supply via **Druzhba** to Germany, leaving the **PCK Schwedt refinery** with roughly **17% less crude**. Germany says petroleum-product security is not in jeopardy because alternative routes exist, but EuroOilWatch is right to stress the broader implication: this is another layer of North West European refining friction landing when Brent is still above $100 and diesel balances are already tight. The good news is that EuroOilWatch's refinery-watch tool says it has detected no thermal anomalies near its tracked 24 major EU and Gulf refineries and terminals in the past 24 hours. (EuroOilWatch)

## What to watch next week

Watch **Hormuz and diplomacy** first. The market is still trading headline to headline, but Reuters' deeper point is that the real issue is not daily price volatility; it is how long inventories can keep absorbing disrupted flows before the system starts to seize.

Watch **jet fuel** second. Europe's replacement imports are still not back to the level the IEA says is needed for comfort, and the pressure is now visible in airline costs and booking behaviour. That makes aviation the most obvious place where a broader fuel-market problem could become visible to the public first.

Watch **diesel and physical crude premiums** third. EuroOilWatch's own numbers suggest the physical market remains tighter than futures, and that gap matters more for logistics operators, procurement teams and industrial users than the Brent headline alone.

## Final word

This week's European fuel story is not crisis over. It is temporary price relief sitting on top of ongoing structural strain. EU-wide average reserve numbers still look acceptable at first glance, but the distribution underneath them is uneven, jet fuel remains the most stressed major product, and regional refining friction is building at the same time as the wider global inventory cushion is being drawn down. Europe may be coping for now. But it is coping by using up margin for error, not by restoring it.
