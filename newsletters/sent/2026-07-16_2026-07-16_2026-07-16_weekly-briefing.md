# Euro Oil Watch Weekly

## Week of 16 July 2026

### Energy Infrastructure Under Attack: Europe Faces a Refined-Fuel Squeeze

**Europe's risk is no longer about how many barrels the world can pump. It is about whether those barrels can be refined, shipped through contested waterways and delivered inland — and on all three counts, the margin is narrowing at once.**

Dear Subscriber,

Europe's energy risk is no longer defined solely by how much crude oil the world can produce. It is increasingly determined by whether those barrels can be refined, loaded onto ships, moved through strategic waterways and delivered to the markets that need them.

Two separate conflicts are now converging on that infrastructure. Ukraine's campaign against Russian refineries is materially reducing the production of petrol, diesel and other refined fuels. At the same time, renewed fighting between the United States and Iran is again threatening tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz.

The result is an unusual — and potentially dangerous — market structure: crude oil may still be available, but the systems required to turn it into usable fuel and deliver it safely are becoming increasingly constrained.

Brent eased slightly as traders took profits after reaching a one-month high during the renewed U.S.–Iran escalation, but the underlying geopolitical premium remains firmly in place. More revealing than the headline price is the shape of the Brent futures curve: the premium for immediate delivery over barrels six months forward widened to about **$8.92 on 14 July**, its steepest since 10 June (Reuters). That steep backwardation is a market warning — near-term supply and delivery conditions are tight even before another major fixed Gulf installation is confirmed destroyed.

## Russia's refining system takes another series of hits

The most clearly verified physical damage of the past fortnight has occurred across Russia's refining and petrochemical network.

### Salavat petrochemical complex

Ukrainian drones struck the Gazprom-owned Salavat complex in Bashkortostan on the night of **13–14 July**. Initial official statements described damage to pipe racks and electrical cables and suggested normal production could resume within days; subsequent industry reports indicated that **two primary refining units were damaged and shut down**, with repairs potentially requiring weeks or months. Salavat processed around 7.2 million tonnes in 2024 — roughly **2.7% of Russian refining** — making it considerably more significant than an ordinary fuel depot. The conflicting recovery estimates are exactly why early official claims should be treated cautiously: a plant need not be destroyed for damage to power supplies, internal pipelines and primary units to halt production across much of the site.

### Saratov refinery

The Saratov refinery stopped processing after a drone attack on **8 July** damaged its **CDU-6 crude-distillation unit** — its only primary unit, handling about 20,000 tonnes of crude a day. Without it, secondary units cannot receive feedstock to make petrol, diesel and fuel oil. Saratov represents roughly **2.2% of Russian refining capacity** and has not been offering products normally on the St Petersburg exchange since the strike.

### Omsk refinery

The Omsk refinery — Russia's largest — halted processing after an attack on **6 July**. It ran approximately **22 million tonnes of crude in 2024** (around a tenth of national capacity), producing roughly five million tonnes of petrol and eight million tonnes of diesel. Its CDU-10 unit, about **38%** of the plant's output, was fire-damaged; CDU-11, another **37%**, was subsequently shut because of network damage. Fuel queues were later reported in Omsk, with some private filling-station networks temporarily restricting sales.

### NORSI and the wider campaign

The NORSI refinery (Lukoil's Nizhny Novgorod plant) also suspended crude processing after its **CDU-6 unit — about 53% of capacity — was damaged**. NORSI is Russia's **second-largest petrol producer**; its disruption alongside Omsk and Saratov has hit three of Russia's most important domestic fuel producers almost simultaneously.

Other sites recently targeted include the Ilsky refinery, the Ust-Luga refining and export complex, the Moscow and TANECO refineries, the Orenburg gas-processing plant, oil depots in the Rostov region, Baltic export facilities such as Vysotsk, and various pipeline and storage installations. Damage varies widely — some attacks caused verified shutdowns, others remain Ukrainian claims without complete independent assessment — but the cumulative effect is no longer marginal.

## Russia has crude — but cannot refine enough of it

The distinction between crude production and refining capacity is now essential. Reuters reported on **16 July** that **nearly 40% of Russian refining capacity is out of operation** — much of it expected to stay down for at least two months — and that Russian buyers are seeking petrol from India, with roughly 60,000 tonnes already at sea and plans to import on the order of 400,000 tonnes a month from several suppliers. (Ukrainian sources put the disabled share nearer 43%.) That "40%" should be read as capacity offline — plants under repair and outages of varying severity — **not** 40% destroyed.

The paradox is that Russian **crude** exports actually rose in June, reaching a post-2022 record — seaborne crude estimated at roughly **4.1–4.4 million barrels a day** across the main trackers (Bloomberg, S&P Global) — even as **refined-product** exports fell to around **1.89 million barrels a day** (Kyiv School of Economics/Kpler; trackers differ on the exact level). Damaged refineries leave more crude available for export while removing diesel and petrol from the world market. Crude availability is therefore a misleading measure of fuel security. Industry reporting suggests operational petrol output has at times covered only about **65% of Russian domestic demand**, prompting export restrictions and a search for imports. The IEA has also trimmed its Russian production forecasts for 2026 and 2027 because of the continuing attacks.

For Europe, the greater danger is not an immediate shortage of crude. It is a tightening global market for the refined products Europe consumes in large quantities but does not produce sufficiently at home.

## Hormuz risk returns

The second pressure point is the Strait of Hormuz. Renewed U.S. strikes on Iranian military facilities, Iranian retaliation and the reinstatement of a naval blockade have again drained commercial confidence in the waterway. Traffic roughly **halved week-on-week to a multi-week low** as operators reassessed risk; independent monitors (JMIC) put the strait at just **4–12 transits a day against a ~138 norm**, threat level SEVERE. Some shipping companies have been unwilling to rely on military escorts after a series of attacks on commercial vessels in and around the strait.

This week the United States also **disabled a vessel attempting to breach the blockade** near Kharg Island, Iran's principal oil-export centre. The important distinction: **the vessel was struck; the Kharg oil terminal itself has not been confirmed hit** in the current sequence. The latest verified U.S. strikes have primarily targeted military, coastal-defence and missile installations — including, for the first time in this round, sites around Tehran — with no credible confirmation yet of a fresh direct strike destroying a major Iranian oilfield, refinery, power station or export terminal.

That may be why Brent has risen into the mid-$80s rather than returning immediately to the extremes seen at the worst of the crisis. The market is pricing the *possibility* of infrastructure destruction — not yet the verified loss of Kharg or another installation of comparable importance.

## The diesel market is flashing the stronger warning

The most important signal for European consumers may not be Brent, but diesel. Russian export restrictions, damaged Russian refineries and the slow recovery of Middle Eastern export plants have driven refined-product margins sharply higher. The IEA reports product cracks and refinery margins climbed to **four-year highs in early July**, with global refinery runs about **six million barrels a day below** their level a year earlier. Diesel has risen sharply — by double digits over the month on European and U.S. benchmarks — reflecting the combined loss of Russian products and renewed disruption around Hormuz, even on days when crude itself slipped.

This matters disproportionately for Europe because diesel is embedded throughout the physical economy — road freight, agriculture, construction and mining, emergency generators, industrial machinery, inland shipping and heating in parts of northern and western Europe. A diesel shock moves through the economy differently from a temporary rise in petrol: it raises the cost of producing and transporting almost everything else.

## Is Europe facing an immediate supply emergency?

Not yet. The European Commission's Energy Union Task Force concluded on **13 July** that there is **no immediate security-of-supply concern for winter 2026–27**: gas-storage targets remain achievable, jet-fuel supply is stable, and Europe has substantial spare LNG-import capacity if additional cargoes can be sourced.

That reassurance matters, but it is not a guarantee of *affordable* energy. Import terminals provide physical capacity; they cannot create LNG cargoes, diesel supplies or safe tanker passages. Europe may be able to acquire the energy it needs while still paying significantly more for it. The immediate risk is therefore better described as a **price and product-availability crisis** than a wholesale loss of supply.

## The quiet European vulnerability: internal transport

Europe is also facing a less dramatic but compounding problem. Low water on the **Rhine** — with the key Kaub gauge below its critical mark, cutting barge loads and pushing Rotterdam-to-inland freight costs sharply higher — and, reportedly, the **Danube**, is restricting the movement of fuel and industrial materials inland from Rotterdam, Antwerp and other import hubs. Europe has grown more dependent on flexible seaborne imports since reducing Russian pipeline supply, but much of that energy must still travel by river, rail, pipeline and road after reaching port. A cargo that arrives safely in Rotterdam does not help if it cannot be moved economically to refineries, depots and industrial centres further inland.

This is how separate disruptions begin to compound: **Russian refinery damage reduces available diesel; Hormuz instability raises shipping and insurance costs; low river levels make European distribution slower and dearer.** None alone necessarily creates a crisis. Together, they narrow Europe's margin for error.

## What EuroOilWatch is watching next

**1. A confirmed strike on Kharg Island.** Kharg handles the majority of Iran's seaborne crude exports. A direct, sustained hit on its loading infrastructure would be a substantial escalation and could move oil sharply higher.

**2. Attacks spreading to Gulf production and export facilities.** The greatest global danger would be a renewed campaign against Saudi, Emirati, Kuwaiti, Qatari or Iraqi oil and gas infrastructure.

**3. The duration of Russian refinery outages.** The difference between a three-day and a three-month shutdown is enormous. Salavat, Omsk, Saratov and NORSI should be tracked by individual processing unit, not by whether the refinery is officially "operating."

**4. Russian fuel-import requirements.** Moscow seeking substantial petrol or diesel imports would confirm the damage has exceeded its domestic reserve and redistribution capacity.

**5. European diesel inventories and physical premiums.** Brent takes the headlines, but diesel stocks, refining margins and physical cargo premiums give the earliest warning of pressure on European transport, farming and industry.

## Bottom line

Europe is not yet facing an absolute absence of oil. It is facing something subtler: deterioration across the infrastructure that converts crude into usable products and carries those products from producer to consumer.

Russia's refinery network is sustaining repeated damage. The Middle East's principal shipping route is again under military pressure. Refined-product markets are considerably tighter than the headline crude balance suggests, while Europe's internal transport system is being challenged by low river levels.

The central question is no longer simply *"Is there enough crude oil in the world?"* It is *"How much crude can be refined, transported and delivered — where it is needed, when it is needed, and at what price?"*

At present, the answer is becoming progressively less comfortable.
