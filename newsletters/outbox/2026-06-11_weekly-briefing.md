**EuroOilWatch Weekly**
**Thursday, 11 June 2026**

Welcome to this week's EuroOilWatch briefing.

The headline oil price looks calmer than it did during the worst of May's surge, but Europe's fuel-security picture is still uncomfortable. EuroOilWatch shows **14 of 27 EU countries with diesel reserves at critical levels** and **jet-fuel shortages affecting 17 of 27 countries**, while **Brent is around $91/barrel** on the dashboard. (Reserve cover is the latest official EU stock data, which carries the usual ~3-month reporting lag — current figures are for March.)

That is the key point this week: Europe is not in a simple "oil price spike" story. It is in a **product-security** story. Diesel and jet fuel are where the pressure reaches logistics, aviation, food movement and industrial supply chains first.

## This week's takeaway

Reuters reports oil jumped earlier today after renewed U.S.–Iran escalation, before paring gains as traders judged whether the latest Hormuz closure claim was translating into actual physical disruption. Brent was around **$93** and WTI around **$90** in early trading, after both gained more than $2 in the session. (EuroOilWatch's own dashboard, refreshed 9 June, reads Brent at **$91.36** — the live screen lags the intraday print.)

Iran has declared the Strait of Hormuz closed and threatened vessels attempting passage. But the market is watching what actually moves, not only what Tehran says — Reuters reports U.S. officials saying commercial ships continued to transit in and out of the Strait. That distinction matters for Europe: a strait can be "open" legally or politically and still be commercially constrained if vessels, insurers, charterers and refiners treat the route as high-risk.

## Three numbers that matter

**Brent crude: ~$91/barrel (dashboard)**
EuroOilWatch's reading puts Brent well below the worst May panic levels, but still high enough to keep pressure on pump prices, freight and refinery margins. Reuters' intraday figure this morning was higher, near $93 after the fresh escalation.

**EU average diesel: €1.804/litre**
Diesel is the practical pressure point for Europe's real economy. The EU average is **€1.804/litre** (EC Weekly Oil Bulletin, 8 June), with **Finland the most expensive at €2.248** and **Malta the cheapest at €1.210**.

**EU jet-fuel cover: 87.3 days**
Jet is the most exposed corner of the system. EuroOilWatch's jet tracker shows the EU average below the 90-day benchmark at **87.3 days**, with **Portugal at 21.2 days**, **Poland at 22.5 days**, and **17 of 27 countries** jet-critical. The ARA commercial jet-fuel hub sits at **600 kt — down 7.6% week on week to a six-year low** in the latest Argus reading (mid-April, the most recent available).

## What changed this week

The most important change is that the market is now separating **headline diplomacy** from **physical supply reality**. Tehran has declared Hormuz closed; the U.S. says commercial ships are still moving. That is exactly the grey zone EuroOilWatch has tracked for weeks — not a clean shutdown, not a clean reopening, but a constrained, expensive, high-risk corridor.

The second change is the latest U.S. inventory signal. Reuters reports U.S. crude inventories fell by **7.2 million barrels** in the week ended 5 June, far more than expected, and that U.S. crude stocks including strategic reserves have fallen by **79 million barrels** since the Iran war began on 28 February. That matters for Europe because American barrels and products have become part of the global replacement system.

The third is that jet fuel is becoming a more visible European vulnerability. Reuters reports Italy — which previously imported around half its annual airline-sector jet-fuel consumption — has lifted domestic production to cover about **70% of demand** in early 2026. A useful adaptation, but it also shows how much effort now goes into simply keeping the aviation system supplied.

## What stands out on EuroOilWatch

The reserve picture is the strongest reason not to be reassured by Brent alone. EU averages show **96 days for petrol**, **89 days for diesel**, and **87 days for jet** (March data). Petrol is just above the benchmark; diesel and jet are not. A headline Brent near $91 can coexist with tight physical diesel, expensive jet fuel, strained commercial inventories and country-level reserve gaps.

The site's active-disruption banner still matters too: EuroOilWatch describes Hormuz as **technically open but commercially constrained** — thin, high-risk movement with AIS gaps — and flags continued pressure from Russia's halt of Kazakh crude via the Druzhba pipeline to Germany since 1 May, which keeps weighing on North West European refining and diesel balances.

## What to watch next week

**Actual Hormuz traffic**, not statements — whether crude, LNG and product vessels move normally, insured, scheduled and at scale.

**Diesel reserves** — with 14 countries at critical diesel levels, diesel is the fuel most likely to hit freight, food distribution and industrial costs first.

**Jet fuel and ARA stocks** — Europe can reroute for a while, but a six-year-low ARA hub is the kind of operational signal that precedes visible shortages (watch for the next Argus update).

**The EC weekly price data** — if pump prices keep easing while physical markets stay tight, consumers get relief; if they stall, the product squeeze is still passing through.

## Final word

This week's message is simple: **do not let a calmer Brent price hide a stressed product market.** Europe is coping, but unevenly. Petrol looks more comfortable than diesel; diesel matters more than crude for the real economy; jet fuel is the sharp edge for summer travel; and Hormuz remains a commercial-risk problem even when some ships move. That is why EuroOilWatch exists — to look beneath the oil headline and track the weaker links before they become obvious.
