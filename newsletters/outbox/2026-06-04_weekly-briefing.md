**EuroOilWatch Weekly**
**Thursday, 4 June 2026**

Welcome to this week's EuroOilWatch briefing.

Europe has had a small price break this week, but it would be a mistake to call it a return to normal. The EuroOilWatch dashboard shows **23 of 27 EU countries in critical status for at least one fuel type**, with EU-average cover at **96.3 days for petrol, 88.8 for diesel and 87.3 for jet fuel** — petrol just above the 90-day benchmark, but diesel and jet now just below it. On the market side the site shows **Brent at $98.50/barrel**, **EU average petrol at €1.812/litre** and **EU average diesel at €1.821/litre** (European Commission Weekly Oil Bulletin, published 4 June, prices to 31 May). (EuroOilWatch; European Commission)

Today's live picture is softer. Reuters had **Brent down around $97 early Thursday — $96.92/bbl at 0458 GMT — with WTI at $95.24** after Israel and Lebanon agreed to implement a ceasefire, raising hopes that wider U.S.–Iran talks could move forward — though the same report noted the IEA warning that global oil stocks could hit critical levels before peak summer demand if current draws continue. (Reuters; IEA)

## This week's takeaway

Prices fell; risk did not. Even if diplomacy improves, oil flows and inventories do not instantly repair themselves. The EIA's public Europe Brent spot series shows how unstable the market remains — $102.75 on 26 May, then $97.11, $95.47, $92.88 and $98.29 on 1 June. The market has not priced in peace; it has priced in the *possibility* of partial de-escalation, while still carrying a large supply-risk premium. (EIA; Reuters)

## Three numbers that matter

**Brent crude — $98.50/barrel.** That's the dashboard reading; Reuters' live print eased to **$96.92** early Thursday (0458 GMT) on ceasefire hopes, while the EIA's Europe Brent spot was **$98.29 for 1 June**. The spot series above — $102.75 down to $92.88 and back to $98.29 inside a week — shows how fast it reverses on a Hormuz headline. (EuroOilWatch; Reuters; EIA)

**EU diesel — €1.821/litre.** Pump prices eased week-on-week (petrol is down to €1.812 too) — welcome relief for hauliers, farmers and tradespeople — but the level is still painful, and euro-area averages run higher still, closer to €1.88–1.90. A few cents of relief is not the same as affordability. (European Commission via EuroOilWatch)

**Jet-fuel cover — 87.3 days.** The thinnest of the three fuels on the dashboard and the genuine operational pressure point. Petrol and diesel are the political story; jet fuel is the one that can break a route. (EuroOilWatch)

## Jet fuel remains Europe's weak point

The IEA says Europe's Middle East jet-fuel arrivals collapsed from **330,000 bpd in March to just 60,000 bpd in April** as the Iran war and the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz cut Gulf supply. Europe leaned harder on the U.S. and Nigeria, but **Kpler data put those replacement flows at only about 221,000 bpd in April** — not enough to fully replace the lost Gulf volume. Reuters reported South Korean jet exports rebounded to ~1.1–1.2 million tonnes in May, easing some Asian pressure, but Europe's aviation system is still riding on longer, costlier replacement routes. Summer travel may look normal until one airport or supplier runs short. (IEA; Reuters citing Kpler)

## The eastern flank is still exposed

Russia's Druzhba crude to Hungary and Slovakia was **restored to about 165,000 bpd in May (after just 55,000 bpd in April)** — but still below the 200,000–235,000 bpd seen before the suspension, and still under an EU sanctions exemption. The sharper warning is for Germany: Kazakhstan's oil transit through the northern Druzhba branch has been **halted since May**, with Russia citing a lack of "technical possibilities," keeping the Schwedt refinery's supply a live vulnerability. Europe's post-2022 strategy hasn't removed pipeline risk — it has changed its shape, from direct Russian crude to dependence on alternative routing, ports and transit politics. (Reuters)

## Europe has a buffer — but it is not a magic shield

EU member states must hold emergency stocks of at least 90 days of net imports (or 61 days of consumption, whichever is higher) and report monthly to the Commission. The latest Eurostat aggregate in its public emergency-stocks article shows the EU holding **108.6 million tonnes of emergency oil stocks in May 2025** — useful context, but not a live 2026 reserve reading. Emergency stocks are designed to prevent *physical* breakdown, not to make prices normal: a release can soften a crisis, it cannot replace lost global flows month after month. (Eurostat)

## What to watch next week

Watch **Brent near $100** first — if it holds there, this week's pump relief fades fast. Watch **jet-fuel replacement flows** (U.S., Nigeria, Asia) carrying Europe's aviation burden. Watch **Druzhba and Schwedt** — Germany's eastern supply is the live pipeline vulnerability. Watch the **next EU Weekly Oil Bulletin** — one week's fall is not a trend. And watch **Hormuz diplomacy** — even a reopening would not instantly restore normal supply chains.

## Final word

Europe has been given a little breathing room, not a solution. Pump prices fell, Brent eased on ceasefire hopes, and replacement flows are improving in places — but inventories are still drawing down, jet fuel remains exposed, and the supply system still rides on long, fragile routes. The danger now is complacency. A lower price this week doesn't mean the shock is over; it means the market is waiting to see whether diplomacy can repair what the war has broken.
