**EuroOilWatch Weekly**
**Thursday, 28 May 2026**

Welcome to this week's EuroOilWatch briefing.

The headline oil price has come down from the worst of May's spike, but Europe's fuel-security picture still looks fragile underneath. The EuroOilWatch dashboard, updated **27 May 2026 at 08:34**, shows **24 of 27 EU countries in critical status for at least one fuel type**. EU average reserves still sit above the 90-day benchmark at **113 days for petrol, 102 days for diesel, and 96 days for jet fuel**, but the market side remains tense: the site shows **Brent at $93.76/barrel**, **EU average petrol at €1.824/litre**, and **EU average diesel at €1.863/litre**. The European Commission's latest Weekly Oil Bulletin was published today, 28 May 2026. (EuroOilWatch; European Commission)

Today's live market picture adds another layer. Reuters reported on 28 May that **crude shot up over 2.5% to $97 a barrel as hostilities escalated after U.S. and Iranian strikes**, with European shares falling as hopes for a quick deal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz weakened. That matters for Europe because it is still an energy importer with thin product buffers in the places that matter most for transport, logistics and aviation. (Reuters)

## Three numbers that matter

**Brent crude — $93.76/barrel.** That is the current EuroOilWatch dashboard reading. It is much lower than the worst panic levels seen earlier in May, but still well above pre-war levels, and today's Reuters reporting suggests the relief move can reverse quickly when Hormuz headlines turn negative again. (EuroOilWatch; Reuters)

**EU average diesel — €1.863/litre.** Diesel remains the most important real-economy number on the site. EuroOilWatch still shows average diesel cover at **102 days**, only modestly above the 90-day benchmark, while its AI analysis says diesel is the most widespread vulnerability, with major economies including **Germany (64.3 days)** and **Spain (68.6 days)** still under clear strain. (EuroOilWatch)

**Physical Northwest European crude — €140/bbl (~$152).** EuroOilWatch's editorial estimate puts prompt-delivery physical crude **62% above Brent futures**, which is a useful reminder that the physical market is still much tighter than the screen price alone suggests. For buyers of real barrels, not paper contracts, that gap matters. (EuroOilWatch · editorial estimate)

## What changed this week

The main shift is that the "deal optimism" that drove oil sharply lower at the start of the week has run into renewed violence and uncertainty. Reuters reported on 27 May that oil fell sharply after reports of a draft U.S.–Iran deal, but by 28 May that optimism had already been hit by fresh strikes and tougher rhetoric. In other words, Europe is still trading a market where diplomacy can knock prices down fast, but physical risk can push them back up just as quickly. (Reuters)

The second shift is that Hormuz still does not look like a normal shipping lane. Reuters reported that **two supertankers and one LNG tanker exited the Strait of Hormuz with their transponders switched off** earlier this week, after weeks of restricted maritime activity. Reuters said pre-war shipping traffic through Hormuz averaged **125 to 140 daily passages**, which is not the profile of a system that has returned to normal. Even when cargoes move, they are moving in a market still shaped by caution, concealment and disruption. (Reuters)

## Why Europe still looks vulnerable

Europe's problem is no longer just the crude headline. It is the combination of uneven product buffers, disrupted trade routes and regional refining friction. EuroOilWatch's active disruption banner still points to the stoppage of **Kazakh crude via Druzhba to Germany**, active since **1 May 2026**, which the site says has cut feedstock to the **PCK Schwedt refinery** by about **17%**. Germany says product security is not in jeopardy, but the site is right to frame this as another layer of friction pressing on North West European diesel balances at the wrong moment. (EuroOilWatch)

Jet fuel remains the other pressure point worth watching closely. EuroOilWatch's jet tracker shows an EU average of **96.4 days**, with **Poland at 25.3 days**, **13 of 27 countries critical**, and the **ARA hub at 600 kt, down 7.6% week on week and at a six-year low**. Europe may avoid a dramatic public shortage headline, but the system is still entering the summer season with noticeably less slack than it would want. (EuroOilWatch)

The damage does not stop at the physical layer. Reuters reported on 28 May from Tokyo that **ECB Chief Economist Philip Lane**, speaking at a BOJ-hosted conference, said the **energy shock from the Iran war would likely have a persistent effect on inflation** even if there is a quick solution to the war. That is a very EuroOilWatch-style point: the first shock is oil price, but the longer tail is replacement cost, restocking pressure and second-round inflation effects. (Reuters citing ECB)

That stress is already visible in European markets. Reuters reported that **Air France and Lufthansa dipped about 1%** each as oil rose again today. The issue is not just aviation profitability; it is that the market still treats Europe's energy-sensitive sectors as exposed whenever Hormuz risk reappears. (Reuters)

## What to watch next week

Watch **Hormuz traffic, not just oil futures**. If more cargoes move quietly but shipping remains restricted and opaque, Europe is still operating in a constrained physical market even if futures wobble lower for a day or two.

Watch **diesel and jet fuel, not just petrol**. EuroOilWatch's current numbers still show those as the system's most meaningful weak points, especially for freight, aviation and industrial activity.

Watch **inflation expectations**. The ECB's message today was that even a quick diplomatic improvement may not undo the energy shock cleanly. That should matter to anyone following procurement, transport costs, or the broader euro-area growth outlook.

## Final word

This week's European fuel story is not "crisis over." It is headline relief sitting on top of unresolved structural strain. Brent on the dashboard is lower. EU-wide average reserve numbers still clear the headline benchmark. But country-level weakness remains widespread, physical crude still trades at a huge premium, Schwedt still matters, Hormuz still looks abnormal, and the ECB is warning that the inflation effects may outlast the military phase of the shock. Europe is coping. It is not comfortable.
