**EuroOilWatch Weekly Briefing**
**Thursday 25 June 2026**

---

European energy markets are calmer than a month ago — but for an awkward reason. The war-risk premium keeps draining out of crude **even as the Strait of Hormuz re-tightens**, leaving prices and the physical picture pointing in opposite directions. Europe is in a less acute position than at the height of the crisis, but the system is still running on thin buffers.

## 1. Prices fall as the risk premium drains

Brent has dropped into the **low-$70s (around $73)**, with **WTI below $70** — its lowest since late February — after a fourth straight session of losses. The market is leaning on the 60-day U.S.–Iran framework, the U.S. licence on Iranian barrels, and strong non-OPEC+ supply (especially the Americas), and is largely looking through the messier physical signals from the Gulf.

For Europe that means modest relief at the pump and on inflation — but it is relief built on *expectations* of normalisation, not on confirmed flows.

## 2. Hormuz: the reopening has stalled

Here is the catch. After a brief recovery — Kpler logged a weekend spike toward ~90 transits (19–21 June) — traffic **reversed**: on 25 June Iran's IRGC reasserted routing control over the Strait, warning vessels to seek clearance and calling deviations "unacceptable and dangerous." Transits collapsed back toward **~5 a day**, with several hundred ships still stranded. The U.S. disputes any closure and points to continued transits.

The metric matters, and the numbers conflict: total ship transits, oil-tanker transits and barrels/day tell different stories, and AIS-dark movements obscure the rest. Some traffic is also tied to *evacuating* stranded vessels and seafarers — not necessarily restored inbound crude liftings. The honest read: **not a clean reopening, and not yet a clean re-closure — a contested, stop-start strait.**

## 3. Inventories remain the real danger

This is where the tightness is real. U.S. commercial crude stocks fell another **6.1 million barrels to 412.1 million** in the week to 19 June — about 7% below the five-year average. The **U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve has dropped to roughly 331 million barrels**, the lowest since 1983, after further releases. Refinery runs are high (utilisation ~96%).

So even with Brent in the $70s, the global buffer is thin: a depleted SPR and below-average commercial stocks mean any fresh shock — a real Hormuz re-closure, a refinery outage, weather — would bite harder than usual. The price calm and the inventory picture are not telling the same story.

## 4. Europe's supply response: Atlantic basin + Venezuela

Europe is **less directly dependent on Hormuz crude than Asia**, but it is exposed through global benchmark prices, refined-product markets (diesel and jet), LNG risk, shipping-insurance costs, and competition for Atlantic-basin barrels. Through the crisis, European refiners leaned harder on U.S., Brazilian and Guyanese supply — supportive for some margins, but keeping product prices above early-2026 levels.

A newer option is **Venezuela**. Crude exports rose to **1.25 million bpd in May** — a seven-year high and the third straight monthly increase — with production around **1.18 million bpd** and stronger flows to the U.S., India and Europe (Caracas is targeting roughly 1.37 million bpd by year-end). For European refiners able to run heavier grades, Venezuelan crude is **a marginal but useful diversification option** — not large enough to transform the supply picture, and still subject to sanctions, quality, financing and political-risk constraints, but one more barrel away from the Gulf. *(Note: this week's M7.5 earthquake near Morón is a humanitarian disaster; major oil-export infrastructure such as the Jose terminal reported normal operations, with El Palito refinery's status still being confirmed — we are monitoring.)*

## 5. Products and refining: diesel and jet still matter

European refinery utilisation has stayed firm as operators maximised runs to rebuild product stocks after the earlier shortages. Diesel and jet fuel remain the squeeze points — they depend on refining capacity, import routes and shipping confidence, not just crude availability. Retail fuel prices have eased modestly with crude but remain above pre-crisis levels in most countries.

## What to watch

- **Hormuz transit data — and which metric:** sustained, *non-Iranian* inbound liftings (not just evacuations or Iranian barrels leaving) would signal a real reopening.
- **Inventories:** continued large U.S. draws and a still-falling SPR keep the system on edge.
- **Venezuelan exports:** further growth adds heavy-crude optionality for Europe (and watch post-quake facility status).
- **Refining margins:** watch for weakening as supply normalises — and for diesel/jet tightness if it doesn't.
- **Geopolitics:** any fresh Hormuz or wider Middle East escalation could reverse the price relief fast.

## Bottom line

Europe is in a less acute position than at the height of the Hormuz crisis — but the system is still thinly buffered and highly sensitive to any setback. The war premium is draining, yet the strait is contested again and inventories are stretched. Europe's diversification strategy — the Americas plus a returning Venezuela — is helping at the margin, but it has not removed chokepoint exposure; it has shifted part of the pressure into Atlantic-basin competition, product markets, insurance costs and inventories. Full normalisation will require not just more visible transits, but restored inbound liftings, lower war-risk costs, fewer AIS-dark movements, and evidence that crude and product stocks are rebuilding.
