# Euro Oil Watch Weekly

## Week of 23 July 2026

### Europe's oil risk is moving beyond Hormuz

**Hormuz remains severely disrupted, Saudi tankers are turning away from the Red Sea, and Europe's diesel and inland-logistics buffers are being tested at the same time.**

Dear Subscriber,

Europe's oil problem is no longer confined to the Strait of Hormuz.

Brent crude rose for a fifth consecutive session on Thursday morning, reaching **$96.49 a barrel** at 06:40 GMT — its highest level since 8 June. West Texas Intermediate climbed to **$88.42**. The rise followed renewed attacks around Hormuz and the extension of the conflict into the Bab el-Mandeb corridor at the southern entrance to the Red Sea.

This matters because Bab el-Mandeb is not simply another vulnerable shipping lane.

It is part of the principal alternative route that Saudi Arabia has used to keep crude moving after traffic through Hormuz was severely curtailed. The work-around is now itself under threat.

Europe is not facing an immediate general shortage of crude or fuel. Refineries are still operating, replacement cargoes are moving and emergency stocks remain available.

But the system is becoming more expensive, slower and less adaptable.

The market is no longer dealing with one blocked route. It is being asked to manage simultaneous pressure on:

* Hormuz
* Bab el-Mandeb
* Russian refining
* European diesel supply
* Rhine and Danube freight capacity
* Strategic and commercial inventories

The danger is not that every part of the system fails at once.

It is that each available workaround is consuming the capacity required to solve the next problem.

## Hormuz remains contested — and severely impaired

Iran's Revolutionary Guards said on Thursday that the Strait of Hormuz was under Iranian control and "completely closed" while American military operations continued.

That is Iran's declaration, not an independently established description of every movement through the strait. Limited traffic has continued during earlier claims of closure, sometimes with vessels sailing without public AIS transmissions.

The physical evidence nevertheless points to severe disruption.

The Revolutionary Guards said one tanker caught fire after an explosion near what they described as a mined route off Oman, while two other vessels turned back. The United States completed a twelfth consecutive night of attacks on Iranian targets.

The important distinction is between **formally closed** and **commercially usable**.

A route may technically remain open while shipowners, charterers, insurers and crews decide that the risk is unacceptable. A handful of controlled or unreported crossings does not restore an energy corridor that previously carried an enormous share of Gulf exports.

For Europe, this means that each replacement barrel must travel farther, cost more or displace another buyer.

## The Red Sea workaround is now under attack

Saudi Arabia responded to the earlier Hormuz disruption by moving much more crude westward through its East–West pipeline to the Red Sea port of Yanbu.

Saudi west-coast exports have averaged approximately **5 million barrels per day since March**, more than twice their pre-war level. Around four-fifths of those shipments then passed through Bab el-Mandeb on their way to Asian buyers, according to Kpler data cited by Reuters.

That route has now become a target.

Yemen's Houthis have declared an attempted blockade of Saudi-linked shipping. Maritime-security reports said the Saudi-flagged tanker *Encelia* was hit in the Red Sea, while the Houthis claimed that warnings forced around ten ships to turn back. Reuters could not independently verify the full Houthi account.

Ships carrying Saudi crude to Asia are already avoiding the southern Red Sea route. Some are instead moving north through Suez, into the Mediterranean and then around Africa.

That is not a minor diversion.

Reuters estimates that avoiding Bab el-Mandeb can add **at least four weeks** to a typical journey and more than double normal sailing times. Very large crude carriers cannot pass fully laden through the Suez Canal and may need to discharge part of their cargo into the Sumed pipeline before reloading north of the canal.

The result is a multiplication of friction:

* Longer voyages
* Higher tanker demand
* More fuel consumed at sea
* Additional insurance costs
* Slower vessel turnaround
* Greater pressure on ports and transfer infrastructure
* Fewer ships available for other routes

The oil has not necessarily disappeared.

The effective carrying capacity of the tanker fleet has.

## Fuel is tightening faster than crude

The most exposed part of the market remains refined products rather than crude oil itself.

Since the beginning of the Iran conflict, benchmark European diesel prices have risen by around **65%**, compared with an approximately **30% increase in Brent crude**, according to Reuters analysis. Diesel refining margins have reached record levels.

The IEA's July Oil Market Report explains why.

Global refinery runs increased in June but remained approximately **6 million barrels per day below** the level recorded a year earlier. Middle Eastern export refineries had not fully restarted, Russian processing had been curtailed by Ukrainian attacks, and Asian refiners continued to operate at reduced rates.

Russia's diesel export ban has removed another important source of supply. Russian diesel and gasoil exports fell to approximately **234,000 barrels per day** in early July, compared with a 2025 average near 817,000 b/d.

Europe does not need to buy Russian diesel directly to feel the effect.

When other buyers lose Russian supply, they compete with Europe for cargoes from the United States, India, the Middle East and Asia. Every cargo diverted elsewhere tightens the Atlantic Basin market.

This is why diesel can continue climbing even when crude briefly stabilises.

A refinery cannot sell a barrel of crude to a truck, tractor, ship or emergency generator. The barrel must first be processed into the correct fuel specification, delivered to the correct terminal and transported to the correct market.

The bottleneck has moved downstream.

## Refining profits confirm where the stress lies

The latest European oil-company results provide a commercial measure of the same shortage.

TotalEnergies reported second-quarter adjusted net income of **$6 billion**, up 67% from a year earlier. Its refining and chemicals division increased adjusted income by 362% to **$1.8 billion**, reflecting exceptionally strong refining margins and trading conditions.

Repsol's adjusted second-quarter profit more than tripled to **€1.84 billion**. Adjusted income from its industrial business rose from €103 million to **€1.24 billion**, while its Spanish refining-margin indicator increased to **$14 a barrel**.

These figures should not be read simply as a story about companies profiting from war.

They identify where scarcity has accumulated.

If crude were the only constraint, the largest earnings effect would remain concentrated upstream. The extraordinary increase in refining income shows that converting oil into usable fuels has become one of the market's narrowest points.

Europe has lost refining capacity over many years on the assumption that global trade could supply any missing product.

The present crisis is testing that assumption.

## European drivers are beginning to see the increase

EuroOilWatch's own calculation from the European Commission Weekly Oil Bulletin — a simple average of the 27 national prices — puts EU petrol at approximately **€1.795 per litre** and diesel at **€1.838**.

The diesel average is now above petrol despite the generally lower tax treatment diesel receives in several European markets. Prices vary substantially by country, but the direction is increasingly broad-based.

These pump prices still contain a delay.

Crude, product premiums, wholesale cargoes and refinery costs take time to move through distribution contracts and station inventories. The latest jump in Brent towards $97 and the renewed Red Sea disruption have therefore not necessarily been fully transmitted to motorists or freight operators.

The immediate European risk is not simply higher family motoring costs.

Diesel is embedded throughout the economy:

* Road haulage
* Agriculture
* Construction
* Mining
* Shipping
* Backup power
* Municipal services
* Food distribution

A diesel rise reaches far beyond the filling station.

It becomes a surcharge on moving almost everything else.

## Demand is already being destroyed

Europe's diesel consumption fell by an estimated **5.7% year on year in May**, to approximately 4.5 million barrels per day, according to preliminary IEA data cited by Reuters.

That does not necessarily mean Europe has suddenly become more efficient.

Some reduction may reflect behavioural change, fuel substitution and electrification. But rapid demand declines during an energy-price shock also indicate weaker industrial activity, fewer freight movements or consumers being priced out of consumption.

According to IEA estimates reported by Reuters, global oil demand fell by nearly 5% year on year in the second quarter, to about 99.1 million b/d.

This is the mechanism by which an apparently manageable energy shortage becomes an economic slowdown:

**Restricted supply → higher fuel prices → reduced transport and production → falling consumption → weaker growth.**

Demand destruction balances the market.

It does not do so painlessly.

## India is providing relief — but not replacing the lost system

India is increasing refined-product exports in response to exceptionally strong margins.

According to Kpler estimates reported by Reuters, Indian exports of light and middle distillates are expected to reach **1.55 million barrels per day in July**, almost double the 866,000 b/d recorded in May and the second-highest level in Kpler records dating to 2017. India has supported those refinery runs with record volumes of Russian crude.

Oman and Taiwan are also increasing exports.

This is evidence that the global market can adapt.

It is not evidence that the shortage has ended.

Even so, total Asian imports of these fuels are still projected at about **5.80 million b/d — 18% below** the three-month pre-conflict average of 7.05 million b/d. There is not enough spare export-refining capacity to replace simultaneously disrupted Middle Eastern and Russian output.

The market is solving part of the problem.

It is doing so by running alternative refineries harder, redirecting crude flows and paying much higher prices for the products that emerge.

## Europe's inland chokepoints remain constrained

Even when a replacement cargo reaches Europe, it must still move inland.

The Rhine and Danube remain unusually low after prolonged heat and drought.

On the Rhine, traders reported tanker barges that could carry around **1,200 tonnes at Duisburg** being restricted to approximately **460 tonnes at Kaub**. Some vessels were operating at as little as 20% of normal capacity, while Rotterdam-to-Karlsruhe tanker rates rose to €60–€70 per tonne from around €45 at the end of June. Rain has lifted levels slightly, but freight costs remain elevated.

On the Danube at Baziaș, Romanian authorities reported flows of approximately **1,700 cubic metres per second**, compared with a normal July average near 4,700. Ferry services have been disrupted, grain barges left idle and reservoir releases managed partly to safeguard cooling requirements at Romania's Cernavodă nuclear plant.

These rivers have not closed.

But their usable freight capacity has contracted.

Europe can therefore face two bottlenecks in sequence:

1. Energy struggles to reach the continent through maritime chokepoints.
2. The energy that does arrive becomes more expensive to distribute through shallow inland waterways.

The tanker reaching Rotterdam is not the end of the supply chain.

It is the beginning of the inland journey.

## The European Commission says supply is secure — for now

The European Commission's Energy Union Task Force said on 13 July that jet-fuel supply remained stable and that Europe faced no immediate security-of-supply concern. EU refineries had increased jet-fuel production and replacement imports had been secured from other regions.

That assessment matters.

Europe is not currently experiencing a generalised physical fuel shortage.

But the Commission also described the position as volatile and estimated that, since February, the Middle East conflict had added approximately **€53 billion** to the EU's collective fossil-fuel import bill.

Since that assessment, the Red Sea threat has intensified and Brent has risen above $96.

The official reassurance and the market warning are not necessarily contradictory.

The Commission is answering: *is sufficient fuel available today?*

The market is answering: *how expensive and difficult is it becoming to maintain that availability?*

Both can be true.

## Brussels is rewriting the oil-stock rules

On 23 July, the European Commission held the second of two workshops on revising the EU Oil Stocks Directive.

The Commission says the Middle East crisis has exposed weaknesses in the existing emergency-stock framework and is considering how to modernise the system. Current law requires member states to hold the greater of 90 days of net imports or 61 days of consumption.

The review is overdue.

The present framework is designed primarily around national stock totals. But the crisis has shown that resilience also depends on:

* Which products are held
* Where they are stored
* Whether they can be released quickly
* Whether the receiving terminals are functioning
* Whether pipelines, rivers, railways and road tankers can distribute them
* Whether several countries need the same replacement supply simultaneously

The Commission notes that oil still represented approximately **38% of the EU energy mix** in 2024, with transport consuming around 70% of the bloc's oil supply. The EU imports roughly **95% of its crude**, although European refineries still produce about **80% of the petroleum products** consumed within the bloc.

This makes the stockholding review more than an administrative exercise.

Europe cannot eliminate its oil exposure by declaring that consumption will eventually fall.

It needs a reserve system capable of functioning during the years before that decline has occurred.

## What to watch next

**Actual vessel movements.** Declarations of closure and blockade matter, but physical tanker movements matter more. Watch whether commercial traffic through Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb continues falling, and whether ships increasingly take the longer route around Africa.

**European diesel premiums.** Diesel remains the fastest transmission channel from the war into European transport, agriculture and industry. A stable Brent price would not end the crisis if gasoil margins remain extreme.

**Refinery utilisation.** The central question is whether Indian, European and other non-Gulf refiners can increase runs enough to compensate for Middle Eastern and Russian losses without triggering outages or maintenance problems.

**Rhine and Danube depth.** Rain can improve conditions rapidly. But continued load restrictions would raise the delivered cost of fuel and industrial inputs even if sufficient cargo reaches European ports.

**Emergency-stock policy.** The EU is now acknowledging that its existing stock architecture requires revision. The most important test will be whether Brussels moves beyond headline national totals towards product-specific, regional and deliverability-based resilience.

## The system is adapting — but every adaptation costs more

Europe's oil system has not failed.

Cargoes are being redirected. India is exporting more fuel. European refineries are running harder. Emergency stocks remain available. Saudi Arabia is using alternative ports, and ships are finding longer routes around threatened waters.

But each solution carries a cost.

Longer voyages consume more ships and fuel. Higher refinery utilisation reduces spare capacity. Road and rail substitution increase diesel demand. Emergency-stock releases leave less protection against the next disruption. Replacement cargoes bid supply away from other buyers.

The central question is therefore no longer whether the market can improvise another workaround.

It is how many workarounds remain — and how much economic damage Europe must accept to keep them operating.

Hormuz exposed Europe's dependence on external chokepoints. The Red Sea escalation is threatening the alternative route. Low water on the Rhine and Danube is constraining distribution inside the continent.

Europe is not running out of oil today.

But the distance between having oil somewhere in the world and delivering the correct fuel to the place that needs it is growing longer, slower and more expensive.

That is the risk now moving through the system.
